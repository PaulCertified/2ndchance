If you have been **declined for life insurance**, you are not alone—and a decline does not always mean you will never qualify. Many people with COPD, heart failure, atrial fibrillation, coronary artery disease, diabetes, or other conditions are still able to obtain coverage through different product types or carriers.

## Why declines happen

Carriers decline or postpone applications when their underwriting guidelines do not match the risk presented. That can be due to a specific diagnosis, recent hospitalization, lab results, height/weight build, or even applying for the wrong type of product (for example, a fully underwritten term policy when a simplified or graded option would be a better fit).

## What to do after a decline

1. **Ask for the reason in writing** — Understanding whether the decision was medical, financial, or related to application answers helps your next steps.
2. **Do not assume every company will say no** — Guidelines vary widely. What one carrier declines, another may approve with a rating or a different product structure.
3. **Consider simplified issue or no-medical-exam options** — These products use fewer health questions and may fit stable, well-managed conditions.
4. **Work with a licensed agent who focuses on impaired risk** — An experienced agent can match you to carriers and products aligned with your health profile.

## Second-chance approval

This is exactly what we mean by a **second chance**: exploring alternatives after a “no” so you can protect your family with a legitimate offer when one exists. **Approval is never guaranteed**; every outcome depends on underwriting and truthful applications.
